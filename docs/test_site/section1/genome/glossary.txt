<dl>
<dd>
<h2><a id="A" name="A">A</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="acquiredgeneticmutation"
name="acquiredgeneticmutation"></a> <b>Acquired genetic
mutation</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#somaticcellgeneticmutation"
target="_self">somatic cell genetic mutation</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="additivegeneticeffects"
name="additivegeneticeffects"></a> <b>Additive genetic
effects</b></dt>

<dd>When the combined effects of alleles at different loci are
equal to the sum of their individual effects.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#anticipation"
target="_self">anticipation</a></u>,<u><a href="#complextrait"
target="_self">complex trait</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="adenine" name="adenine"></a> <b>Adenine</b> (A)</dt>

<dd>A nitrogenous base, one member of the base pair AT
(adenine-thymine).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#basepair" target="_self">base
pair</a></u>,<u><a href="#nucleotide"
target="_self">nucleotide</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="affectedrelativepair" name="affectedrelativepair"></a>
<b>Affected relative pair</b></dt>

<dd>Individuals related by blood, each of whom is affected with the
same trait. Examples are affected sibling, cousin, and avuncular
pairs.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#avuncularrelationship"
target="_self">avuncular relationship</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="aggregationtechnique" name="aggregationtechnique"></a>
<b>Aggregation technique</b></dt>

<dd>A technique used in model organism studies in which embryos at
the 8-cell stage of development are pushed together to yield a
single embryo (used as an alternative to microinjection).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#modelorganisms" target="_self">model
organisms</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="allele" name="allele"></a> <b>Allele</b></dt>

<dd>Alternative form of a genetic locus; a single allele for each
locus is inherited from each parent (e.g., at a locus for eye color
the allele might result in blue or brown eyes).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#locus"
target="_self">locus</a></u>,<u><a href="#geneexpression"
target="_self">gene expression</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="allogeneic" name="allogeneic"></a>
<b>Allogeneic</b></dt>

<dd>Variation in alleles among members of the same species.</dd>

<dt><a id="alternativesplicing" name="alternativesplicing"></a>
<b>Alternative splicing</b></dt>

<dd>Different ways of combining a gene's exons to make variants of
the complete protein</dd>

<dt><a id="aminoacid" name="aminoacid"></a> <b>Amino acid</b></dt>

<dd>Any of a class of 20 molecules that are combined to form
proteins in living things. The sequence of amino acids in a protein
and hence protein function are determined by the genetic code.</dd>

<dt><a id="amplification" name="amplification"></a>
<b>Amplification</b></dt>

<dd>An increase in the number of copies of a specific DNA fragment;
can be in vivo or in vitro.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloning"
target="_self">cloning</a></u>,<u><a
href="#polymerasechainreaction" target="_self">polymerase chain
reaction</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="animalmodel" name="animalmodel"></a> <b>Animal
model</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#modelorganisms" target="_self">model
organisms</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="annotation" name="annotation"></a>
<b>Annotation</b></dt>

<dd>Adding pertinent information such as gene coded for, amino acid
sequence, or other commentary to the database entry of raw sequence
of DNA bases.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#bioinformatics"
target="_self">bioinformatics</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="anticipation" name="anticipation"></a>
<b>Anticipation</b></dt>

<dd>Each generation of offspring has increased severity of a
genetic disorder; e.g., a grandchild may have earlier onset and
more severe symptoms than the parent, who had earlier onset than
the grandparent.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#additivegeneticeffects"
target="_self">additive genetic effects</a></u>,<u><a
href="#complextrait" target="_self">complex trait</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="antisense" name="antisense"></a> <b>Antisense</b></dt>

<dd>Nucleic acid that has a sequence exactly opposite to an mRNA
molecule made by the body; binds to the mRNA molecule to prevent a
protein from being made.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#transcription"
target="_self">transcription</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="apoptosis" name="apoptosis"></a> <b>Apoptosis</b></dt>

<dd>Programmed cell death, the body's normal method of disposing of
damaged, unwanted, or unneeded cells.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cell"
target="_self">cell</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="arrayedlibrary" name="arrayedlibrary"></a> <b>Arrayed
library</b></dt>

<dd>Individual primary recombinant clones (hosted in phage, cosmid,
YAC, or other vector) that are placed in two-dimensional arrays in
microtiter dishes. Each primary clone can be identified by the
identity of the plate and the clone location (row and column) on
that plate. Arrayed libraries of clones can be used for many
applications, including screening for a specific gene or genomic
region of interest.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#library"
target="_self">library</a></u>,<u><a href="#genomiclibrary"
target="_self">genomic library</a></u>,<u><a
href="#genechiptechnology" target="_self">gene chip
technology</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="assembly" name="assembly"></a> <b>Assembly</b></dt>

<dd>Putting sequenced fragments of DNA into their correct
chromosomal positions.</dd>

<dt><a id="autoradiography" name="autoradiography"></a>
<b>Autoradiography</b></dt>

<dd>A technique that uses X-ray film to visualize radioactively
labeled molecules or fragments of molecules; used in analyzing
length and number of DNA fragments after they are separated by gel
electrophoresis.</dd>

<dt><a id="autosomaldominant" name="autosomaldominant"></a>
<b>Autosomal dominant</b></dt>

<dd>A gene on one of the non-sex chromosomes that is always
expressed, even if only one copy is present. The chance of passing
the gene to offspring is 50% for each pregnancy.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#autosome"
target="_self">autosome</a></u>,<u><a href="#dominant"
target="_self">dominant</a></u>,<u><a href="#gene"
target="_self">gene</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="autosome" name="autosome"></a> <b>Autosome</b></dt>

<dd>A chromosome not involved in sex determination. The diploid
human genome consists of a total of 46 chromosomes: 22 pairs of
autosomes, and 1 pair of sex chromosomes (the X and Y
chromosomes).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#sexchromosome" target="_self">sex
chromosome</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="avuncularrelationship" name="avuncularrelationship"></a>
<b>Avuncular relationship</b></dt>

<dd>The genetic relationship between nieces and nephews and their
aunts and uncles. 
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="B" name="B">B</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="backcross" name="backcross"></a> <b>Backcross</b></dt>

<dd>A cross between an animal that is heterozygous for alleles
obtained from two parental strains and a second animal from one of
those parental strains. Also used to describe the breeding protocol
of an outcross followed by a backcross.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#modelorganisms" target="_self">model
organisms</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="bacterialartificialchromosome"
name="bacterialartificialchromosome"></a> <b>Bacterial artificial
chromosome</b> (BAC)</dt>

<dd>A vector used to clone DNA fragments (100- to 300-kb insert
size; average, 150 kb) in <i>Escherichia coli</i> cells. Based on
naturally occurring F-factor plasmid found in the bacterium <i>E.
coli</i>.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="bacteriophage" name="bacteriophage"></a>
<b>Bacteriophage</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#phage"
target="_self">phage</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="base" name="base"></a> <b>Base</b></dt>

<dd>One of the molecules that form DNA and RNA molecules.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#nucleotide"
target="_self">nucleotide</a></u>,<u><a href="#basepair"
target="_self">base pair</a></u>,<u><a href="#basesequence"
target="_self">base sequence</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="basepair" name="basepair"></a> <b>Base pair</b>
(bp)</dt>

<dd>Two nitrogenous bases (adenine and thymine or guanine and
cytosine) held together by weak bonds. Two strands of DNA are held
together in the shape of a double helix by the bonds between base
pairs.</dd>

<dt><a id="basesequence" name="basesequence"></a> <b>Base
sequence</b></dt>

<dd>The order of nucleotide bases in a DNA molecule; determines
structure of proteins encoded by that DNA.</dd>

<dt><a id="basesequenceanalysis" name="basesequenceanalysis"></a>
<b>Base sequence analysis</b></dt>

<dd>A method, sometimes automated, for determining the base
sequence.</dd>

<dt><a id="behavioralgenetics" name="behavioralgenetics"></a>
<b>Behavioral genetics</b></dt>

<dd>The study of genes that may influence behavior.</dd>

<dt><a id="bioinformatics" name="bioinformatics"></a>
<b>Bioinformatics</b></dt>

<dd>The science of managing and analyzing biological data using
advanced computing techniques. Especially important in analyzing
genomic research data.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#informatics"
target="_self">informatics</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="bioremediation" name="bioremediation"></a>
<b>Bioremediation</b></dt>

<dd>The use of biological organisms such as plants or microbes to
aid in removing hazardous substances from an area.</dd>

<dt><a id="biotechnology" name="biotechnology"></a>
<b>Biotechnology</b></dt>

<dd>A set of biological techniques developed through basic research
and now applied to research and product development. In particular,
biotechnology refers to the use by industry of recombinant DNA,
cell fusion, and new bioprocessing techniques.</dd>

<dt><a id="birthdefect" name="birthdefect"></a> <b>Birth
defect</b></dt>

<dd>Any harmful trait, physical or biochemical, present at birth,
whether a result of a genetic mutation or some other nongenetic
factor.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#congenital"
target="_self">congenital</a></u>,<u><a href="#gene"
target="_self">gene</a></u>,<u><a href="#mutation"
target="_self">mutation</a></u>,<u><a href="#syndrome"
target="_self">syndrome</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="blast" name="blast"></a> <b>BLAST</b></dt>

<dd>A computer program that identifies homologous (similar) genes
in different organisms, such as human, fruit fly, or nematode. 
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="C" name="C">C</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="cancer" name="cancer"></a> <b>Cancer</b></dt>

<dd>Diseases in which abnormal cells divide and grow unchecked.
Cancer can spread from its original site to other parts of the body
and can be fatal.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#hereditarycancer"
target="_self">hereditary cancer</a></u>,<u><a
href="#sporadiccancer" target="_self">sporadic cancer</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="candidategene" name="candidategene"></a> <b>Candidate
gene</b></dt>

<dd>A gene located in a chromosome region suspected of being
involved in a disease.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#positionalcloning"
target="_self">positional cloning</a></u>,<u><a href="#protein"
target="_self">protein</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="capillaryarray" name="capillaryarray"></a> <b>Capillary
array</b></dt>

<dd>Gel-filled silica capillaries used to separate fragments for
DNA sequencing. The small diameter of the capillaries permit the
application of higher electric fields, providing high speed, high
throughput separations that are significantly faster than
traditional slab gels.</dd>

<dt><a id="carcinogen" name="carcinogen"></a>
<b>Carcinogen</b></dt>

<dd>Something which causes cancer to occur by causing changes in a
cell's DNA.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mutagene"
target="_self">mutagene</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="carrier" name="carrier"></a> <b>Carrier</b></dt>

<dd>An individual who possesses an unexpressed, recessive
trait.</dd>

<dt><a id="cdnalibrary" name="cdnalibrary"></a> <b>cDNA
library</b></dt>

<dd>A collection of DNA sequences that code for genes. The
sequences are generated in the laboratory from mRNA
sequences.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#messengerrna"
target="_self">messenger RNA</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="cell" name="cell"></a> <b>Cell</b></dt>

<dd>The basic unit of any living organism that carries on the
biochemical processes of life.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#genome"
target="_self">genome</a></u>,<u><a href="#nucleus"
target="_self">nucleus</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="centimorgan" name="centimorgan"></a> <b>Centimorgan</b>
(cM)</dt>

<dd>A unit of measure of recombination frequency. One centimorgan
is equal to a 1% chance that a marker at one genetic locus will be
separated from a marker at a second locus due to crossing over in a
single generation. In human beings, one centimorgan is equivalent,
on average, to one million base pairs.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#megabase"
target="_self">megabase</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="centromere" name="centromere"></a>
<b>Centromere</b></dt>

<dd>A specialized chromosome region to which spindle fibers attach
during cell division.</dd>

<dt><a id="chimera(pl.chimaera)" name="chimera(pl.chimaera)"></a>
<b>Chimera (pl. chimaera)</b></dt>

<dd>An organism that contains cells or tissues with a different
genotype. These can be mutated cells of the host organism or cells
from a different organism or species.</dd>

<dt><a id="chimeraplasty" name="chimeraplasty"></a>
<b>Chimeraplasty</b></dt>

<dd>An experimental targeted repair process in which a desirable
sequence of DNA is combined with RNA to form a chimeraplast. These
molecules bind selectively to the target DNA. Once bound, the
chimeraplast activates a naturally occurring gene-correcting
mechanism. Does not use viral or other conventional gene-delivery
vectors.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#genetherapy" target="_self">gene
therapy</a></u>,<u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="chloroplastchromosome" name="chloroplastchromosome"></a>
<b>Chloroplast chromosome</b></dt>

<dd>Circular DNA found in the photosynthesizing organelle
(chloroplast) of plants instead of the cell nucleus where most
genetic material is located.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromomere" name="chromomere"></a>
<b>Chromomere</b></dt>

<dd>One of the serially aligned beads or granules of a eukaryotic
chromosome, resulting from local coiling of a continuous DNA
thread.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosomaldeletion" name="chromosomaldeletion"></a>
<b>Chromosomal deletion</b></dt>

<dd>The loss of part of a chromosome's DNA.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosomalinversion" name="chromosomalinversion"></a>
<b>Chromosomal inversion</b></dt>

<dd>Chromosome segments that have been turned 180 degrees. The gene
sequence for the segment is reversed with respect to the rest of
the chromosome.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosome" name="chromosome"></a>
<b>Chromosome</b></dt>

<dd>The self-replicating genetic structure of cells containing the
cellular DNA that bears in its nucleotide sequence the linear array
of genes. In prokaryotes, chromosomal DNA is circular, and the
entire genome is carried on one chromosome. Eukaryotic genomes
consist of a number of chromosomes whose DNA is associated with
different kinds of proteins.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosomepainting" name="chromosomepainting"></a>
<b>Chromosome painting</b></dt>

<dd>Attachment of certain fluorescent dyes to targeted parts of the
chromosome. Used as a diagnositic for particular diseases, e.g.
types of leukemia.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosomeregionp" name="chromosomeregionp"></a>
<b>Chromosome region p</b></dt>

<dd>A designation for the short arm of a chromosome.</dd>

<dt><a id="chromosomeregionq" name="chromosomeregionq"></a>
<b>Chromosome region q</b></dt>

<dd>A designation for the long arm of a chromosome.</dd>

<dt><a id="clone" name="clone"></a> <b>Clone</b></dt>

<dd>An exact copy made of biological material such as a DNA segment
(e.g., a gene or other region), a whole cell, or a complete
organism.</dd>

<dt><a id="clonebank" name="clonebank"></a> <b>Clone bank</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#genomiclibrary" target="_self">genomic
library</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="cloning" name="cloning"></a> <b>Cloning</b></dt>

<dd>Using specialized DNA technology to produce multiple, exact
copies of a single gene or other segment of DNA to obtain enough
material for further study. This process, used by researchers in
the Human Genome Project, is referred to as cloning DNA.&nbsp; The
resulting cloned (copied) collections of DNA molecules are called
clone libraries. A second type of cloning exploits the natural
process of cell division to make many copies of an entire cell. The
genetic makeup of these cloned cells, called a cell line, is
identical to the original cell. A third type of cloning produces
complete, genetically identical animals such as the famous Scottish
sheep, Dolly.&nbsp;<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="cloningvector" name="cloningvector"></a> <b>Cloning
vector</b></dt>

<dd>DNA molecule originating from a virus, a plasmid, or the cell
of a higher organism into which another DNA fragment of appropriate
size can be integrated without loss of the vector's capacity for
self-replication; vectors introduce foreign DNA into host cells,
where the DNA can be reproduced in large quantities. Examples are
plasmids, cosmids, and yeast artificial chromosomes; vectors are
often recombinant molecules containing DNA sequences from several
sources.</dd>

<dt><a id="code" name="code"></a> <b>Code</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#geneticcode" target="_self">genetic
code</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="codominance" name="codominance"></a>
<b>Codominance</b></dt>

<dd>Situation in which two different alleles for a genetic trait
are both expressed.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#autosomaldominant"
target="_self">autosomal dominant</a></u>,<u><a
href="#recessivegene" target="_self">recessive gene</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="codon" name="codon"></a> <b>Codon</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#geneticcode" target="_self">genetic
code</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="coisogenicorcongenic" name="coisogenicorcongenic"></a>
<b>Coisogenic or congenic</b></dt>

<dd>Nearly identical strains of an organism; they vary at only a
single locus.</dd>

<dt><a id="comparativegenomics" name="comparativegenomics"></a>
<b>Comparative genomics</b></dt>

<dd>The study of human genetics by comparisons with model organisms
such as mice, the fruit fly, and the bacterium <i>E. coli</i>.</dd>

<dt><a id="complementarydna" name="complementarydna"></a>
<b>Complementary DNA</b> (cDNA)</dt>

<dd>DNA that is synthesized in the laboratory from a messenger RNA
template.</dd>

<dt><a id="complementarysequence" name="complementarysequence"></a>
<b>Complementary sequence</b></dt>

<dd>Nucleic acid base sequence that can form a double-stranded
structure with another DNA fragment by following base-pairing rules
(A pairs with T and C with G). The complementary sequence to GTAC
for example, is CATG.</dd>

<dt><a id="complextrait" name="complextrait"></a> <b>Complex
trait</b></dt>

<dd>Trait that has a genetic component that does not follow strict
Mendelian inheritance. May involve the interaction of two or more
genes or gene-environment interactions.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mendelianinheritance"
target="_self">Mendelian inheritance</a></u>,<u><a
href="#additivegeneticeffects" target="_self">additive genetic
effects</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="computationalbiology" name="computationalbiology"></a>
<b>Computational biology</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#bioinformatics"
target="_self">bioinformatics</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="confidentiality" name="confidentiality"></a>
<b>Confidentiality</b></dt>

<dd>In genetics, the expectation that genetic material and the
information gained from testing that material will not be available
without the donor's consent.</dd>

<dt><a id="congenital" name="congenital"></a>
<b>Congenital</b></dt>

<dd>Any trait present at birth, whether the result of a genetic or
nongenetic factor.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#birthdefect" target="_self">birth
defect</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="conservedsequence" name="conservedsequence"></a>
<b>Conserved sequence</b></dt>

<dd>A base sequence in a DNA molecule (or an amino acid sequence in
a protein) that has remained essentially unchanged throughout
evolution.</dd>

<dt><a id="constitutiveablation" name="constitutiveablation"></a>
<b>Constitutive ablation</b></dt>

<dd>Gene expression that results in cell death.</dd>

<dt><a id="contig" name="contig"></a> <b>Contig</b></dt>

<dd>Group of cloned (copied) pieces of DNA representing overlapping
regions of a particular chromosome.</dd>

<dt><a id="contigmap" name="contigmap"></a> <b>Contig map</b></dt>

<dd>A map depicting the relative order of a linked library of
overlapping clones representing a complete chromosomal
segment.</dd>

<dt><a id="cosmid" name="cosmid"></a> <b>Cosmid</b></dt>

<dd>Artificially constructed cloning vector containing the cos gene
of phage lambda. Cosmids can be packaged in lambda phage particles
for infection into <i>E. coli</i>; this permits cloning of larger
DNA fragments (up to 45kb) than can be introduced into bacterial
hosts in plasmid vectors.</dd>

<dt><a id="crossingover" name="crossingover"></a> <b>Crossing
over</b></dt>

<dd>The breaking during meiosis of one maternal and one paternal
chromosome, the exchange of corresponding sections of DNA, and the
rejoining of the chromosomes. This process can result in an
exchange of alleles between chromosomes.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#recombination"
target="_self">recombination</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="cytogenetics" name="cytogenetics"></a>

<dt><a id="linkage" name="linkage"></a> <b>Linkage</b></dt>

<dd>The proximity of two or more markers (e.g., genes, RFLP

<dt><a id="locus(pl.loci)" name="locus(pl.loci)"></a> <b>Locus (pl.

<dt><a id="polygenicdisorder" name="polygenicdisorder"></a>
<b>Polygenic disorder</b></dt>

<dd>Genetic disorder resulting from the combined action of alleles

<dt><a id="polymerasechainreaction"
name="polymerasechainreaction"></a> <b>Polymerase chain
reaction</b> (PCR)</dt>

<dd>A method for amplifying a DNA base sequence using a heat-stable

strand at one end of the sequence to be amplified and one
complementary to the (-) strand at the other end. Because the newly
synthesized DNA strands can subsequently serve as additional
templates for the same primer sequences, successive rounds of
primer annealing, strand elongation, and dissociation produce rapid
and highly specific amplification of the desired sequence. PCR also
can be used to detect the existence of the defined sequence in a
DNA sample.</dd>

<dt><a id="polymerase,dnaorrna" name="polymerase,dnaorrna"></a>

<dt><a id="polymorphism" name="polymorphism"></a>
<b>Polymorphism</b></dt>

<dt><a id="sequencetaggedsite" name="sequencetaggedsite"></a>

<dd>Short (200 to 500 base pairs) DNA sequence that has a single

tags (ESTs) are STSs derived from cDNAs.</dd>

researchers use both random (or shotgun) and directed strategies in
combination to sequence the human genome.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#library"
target="_self">library</a></u>,<u><a href="#genomiclibrary"
target="_self">genomic library</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="singlenucleotidepolymorphism"

<dd>DNA sequence variations that occur when a single nucleotide (A,

target="_self">polymorphism</a></u>,<u><a
href="#single-genedisorder" target="_self">single-gene
disorder</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="single-genedisorder" name="single-genedisorder"></a>
<b>Single-gene disorder</b></dt>

<dd>Hereditary disorder caused by a mutant allele of a single gene
(e.g., Duchenne muscular dystrophy, retinoblastoma, sickle cell
disease).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#polygenicdisorders"
target="_self">polygenic disorders</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="somaticcell" name="somaticcell"></a> <b>Somatic
cell</b></dt>

<dd>Any cell in the body except gametes and their precursors.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#gamete"
target="_self">gamete</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="somaticcellgenetherapy"
name="somaticcellgenetherapy"></a> <b>Somatic cell gene
therapy</b></dt>

<dd>Incorporating new genetic material into cells for therapeutic
purposes. The new genetic material cannot be passed to
offspring.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#genetherapy" target="_self">gene
therapy</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="somaticcellgeneticmutation"
name="somaticcellgeneticmutation"></a> <b>Somatic cell genetic
mutation</b></dt>

<dd>A change in the genetic structure that is neither inherited nor
passed to offspring. Also called acquired mutations.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#germlinegeneticmutation"
target="_self">germ line genetic mutation</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="southernblotting" name="southernblotting"></a>
<b>Southern blotting</b></dt>

<dd>Transfer by absorption of DNA fragments separated in
electrophoretic gels to membrane filters for detection of specific
base sequences by radio-labeled complementary probes.</dd>

<dt><a id="spectralkaryotype" name="spectralkaryotype"></a>
<b>Spectral karyotype</b> (SKY)</dt>

<dd>A graphic of all an organism's chromosomes, each labeled with a
different color. Useful for identifying chromosomal
abnormalities.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#chromosome"
target="_self">chromosome</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="splicesite" name="splicesite"></a> <b>Splice
site</b></dt>

<dd>Location in the DNA sequence where RNA removes the noncoding
areas to form a continuous gene transcript for translation into a
protein.</dd>

<dt><a id="sporadiccancer" name="sporadiccancer"></a> <b>Sporadic
cancer</b></dt>

<dd>Cancer that occurs randomly and is not inherited from parents.
Caused by DNA changes in one cell that grows and divides, spreading
throughout the body.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#hereditarycancer"
target="_self">hereditary cancer</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="stemcell" name="stemcell"></a> <b>Stem cell</b></dt>

<dd>Undifferentiated, primitive cells in the bone marrow that have
the ability both to multiply and to differentiate into specific
blood cells.</dd>

<dt><a id="structuralgenomics" name="structuralgenomics"></a>
<b>Structural genomics</b></dt>

<dd>The effort to determine the 3D structures of large numbers of
proteins using both experimental techniques and computer
simulation</dd>

<dt><a id="substitution" name="substitution"></a>
<b>Substitution</b></dt>

<dd>In genetics, a type of mutation due to replacement of one
nucleotide in a DNA sequence by another nucleotide or replacement
of one amino acid in a protein by another amino acid.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mutation"
target="_self">mutation</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="suppressorgene" name="suppressorgene"></a> <b>Suppressor
gene</b></dt>

<dd>A gene that can suppress the action of another gene.</dd>

<dt><a id="syndrome" name="syndrome"></a> <b>Syndrome</b></dt>

<dd>The group or recognizable pattern of symptoms or abnormalities
that indicate a particular trait or disease.</dd>

<dt><a id="syngeneic" name="syngeneic"></a> <b>Syngeneic</b></dt>

<dd>Genetically identical members of the same species.</dd>

<dt><a id="synteny" name="synteny"></a> <b>Synteny</b></dt>

<dd>Genes occurring in the same order on chromosomes of different
species.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#linkage"
target="_self">linkage</a></u>,<u><a href="#conservedsequence"
target="_self">conserved sequence</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="T" name="T">T</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="tandemrepeatsequences" name="tandemrepeatsequences"></a>
<b>Tandem repeat sequences</b></dt>

<dd>Multiple copies of the same base sequence on a chromosome; used
as markers in physical mapping.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#physicalmap" target="_self">physical
map</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="targetedmutagenesis" name="targetedmutagenesis"></a>
<b>Targeted mutagenesis</b></dt>

<dd>Deliberate change in the genetic structure directed at a
specific site on the chromosome. Used in research to determine the
targeted region's function.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mutation"
target="_self">mutation</a></u>,<u><a href="#polymorphism"
target="_self">polymorphism</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="technologytransfer" name="technologytransfer"></a>
<b>Technology transfer</b></dt>

<dd>The process of transferring scientific findings from research
laboratories to the commercial sector.</dd>

<dt><a id="telomerase" name="telomerase"></a>
<b>Telomerase</b></dt>

<dd>The enzyme that directs the replication of telomeres.</dd>

<dt><a id="telomere" name="telomere"></a> <b>Telomere</b></dt>

<dd>The end of a chromosome. This specialized structure is involved
in the replication and stability of linear DNA molecules.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#dnareplication" target="_self">DNA
replication</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="teratogenic" name="teratogenic"></a>
<b>Teratogenic</b></dt>

<dd>Substances such as chemicals or radiation that cause abnormal
development of a embryo.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mutatgen"
target="_self">mutatgen</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="thymine" name="thymine"></a> <b>Thymine</b> (T)</dt>

<dd>A nitrogenous base, one member of the base pair AT
(adenine-thymine).<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#basepair" target="_self">base
pair</a></u>,<u><a href="#nucleotide"
target="_self">nucleotide</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="toxicogenomics" name="toxicogenomics"></a>
<b>Toxicogenomics</b></dt>

<dd>The study of how genomes respond to environmental stressors or
toxicants. Combines genome-wide mRNA expression profiling with
protein expression patterns using bioinformatics to understand the
role of gene-environment interactions in disease and
dysfunction.</dd>

<dt><a id="transcription" name="transcription"></a>
<b>Transcription</b></dt>

<dd>The synthesis of an RNA copy from a sequence of DNA (a gene);
the first step in gene expression.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#translation"
target="_self">translation</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="transcriptionfactor" name="transcriptionfactor"></a>
<b>Transcription factor</b></dt>

<dd>A protein that binds to regulatory regions and helps control
gene expression.</dd>

<dt><a id="transcriptome" name="transcriptome"></a>
<b>Transcriptome</b></dt>

<dd>The full complement of activated genes, mRNAs, or transcripts
in a particular tissue at a particular time</dd>

<dt><a id="transfection" name="transfection"></a>
<b>Transfection</b></dt>

<dd>The introduction of foreign DNA into a host cell.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u>,<u><a href="#genetherapy" target="_self">gene
therapy</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="transferrna" name="transferrna"></a> <b>Transfer RNA</b>
(tRNA)</dt>

<dd>A class of RNA having structures with triplet nucleotide
sequences that are complementary to the triplet nucleotide coding
sequences of mRNA. The role of tRNAs in protein synthesis is to
bond with amino acids and transfer them to the ribosomes, where
proteins are assembled according to the genetic code carried by
mRNA.</dd>

<dt><a id="transformation" name="transformation"></a>
<b>Transformation</b></dt>

<dd>A process by which the genetic material carried by an
individual cell is altered by incorporation of exogenous DNA into
its genome.</dd>

<dt><a id="transgenic" name="transgenic"></a>
<b>Transgenic</b></dt>

<dd>An experimentally produced organism in which DNA has been
artificially introduced and incorporated into the organism's germ
line.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cell"
target="_self">cell</a></u>,<u><a href="#dna"
target="_self">DNA</a></u>,<u><a href="#gene"
target="_self">gene</a></u>,<u><a href="#nucleus"
target="_self">nucleus</a></u>,<u><a href="#germline"
target="_self">germ line</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="translation" name="translation"></a>
<b>Translation</b></dt>

<dd>The process in which the genetic code carried by mRNA directs
the synthesis of proteins from amino acids.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#transcription"
target="_self">transcription</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="translocation" name="translocation"></a>
<b>Translocation</b></dt>

<dd>A mutation in which a large segment of one chromosome breaks
off and attaches to another chromosome.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#mutation"
target="_self">mutation</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="transposableelement" name="transposableelement"></a>
<b>Transposable element</b></dt>

<dd>A class of DNA sequences that can move from one chromosomal
site to another.</dd>

<dt><a id="trisomy" name="trisomy"></a> <b>Trisomy</b></dt>

<dd>Possessing three copies of a particular chromosome instead of
the normal two copies.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cell"
target="_self">cell</a></u>,<u><a href="#gene"
target="_self">gene</a></u>,<u><a href="#geneexpression"
target="_self">gene expression</a></u>,<u><a href="#chromosome"
target="_self">chromosome</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="U" name="U">U</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="uracil" name="uracil"></a> <b>Uracil</b></dt>

<dd>A nitrogenous base normally found in RNA but not DNA; uracil is
capable of forming a base pair with adenine.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#basepair" target="_self">base
pair</a></u>,<u><a href="#nucleotide"
target="_self">nucleotide</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="V" name="V">V</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="vector" name="vector"></a> <b>Vector</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="virus" name="virus"></a> <b>Virus</b></dt>

<dd>A noncellular biological entity that can reproduce only within
a host cell. Viruses consist of nucleic acid covered by protein;
some animal viruses are also surrounded by membrane. Inside the
infected cell, the virus uses the synthetic capability of the host
to produce progeny virus.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="W" name="W">W</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="westernblot" name="westernblot"></a> <b>Western
blot</b></dt>

<dd>A technique used to identify and locate proteins based on their
ability to bind to specific antibodies.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#dna" target="_self">DNA</a></u>,<u><a
href="#northernblot" target="_self">Northern blot</a></u>,<u><a
href="#protein" target="_self">protein</a></u>,<u><a href="#rna"
target="_self">RNA</a></u>,<u><a href="#southernblotting"
target="_self">Southern blotting</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="wildtype" name="wildtype"></a> <b>Wild type</b></dt>

<dd>The form of an organism that occurs most frequently in
nature.</dd>

<dt><a id="workingdraftdnasequence"
name="workingdraftdnasequence"></a> <b>Working Draft DNA
Sequence</b></dt>

<dd><i>See:</i> <u><a href="#draftdnasequence" target="_self">Draft
DNA Sequence</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="X" name="X">X</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="xchromosome" name="xchromosome"></a> <b>X
chromosome</b></dt>

<dd>One of the two sex chromosomes, X and Y.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#ychromosome" target="_self">Y
chromosome</a></u>,<u><a href="#sexchromosome" target="_self">sex
chromosome</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="xenograft" name="xenograft"></a> <b>Xenograft</b></dt>

<dd>Tissue or organs from an individual of one species transplanted
into or grafted onto an organism of another species, genus, or
family. A common example is the use of pig heart valves in humans. 
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="Y" name="Y">Y</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="ychromosome" name="ychromosome"></a> <b>Y
chromosome</b></dt>

<dd>One of the two sex chromosomes, X and Y.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#xchromosome" target="_self">X
chromosome</a></u>,<u><a href="#sexchromosome" target="_self">sex
chromosome</a></u><br />
</dd>

<dt><a id="yeastartificialchromosome"
name="yeastartificialchromosome"></a> <b>Yeast artificial
chromosome</b> (YAC)</dt>

<dd>Constructed from yeast DNA, it is a vector used to clone large
DNA fragments.<br />
<i>See also:</i> <u><a href="#cloningvector" target="_self">cloning
vector</a></u>,<u><a href="#cosmid"
target="_self">cosmid</a></u><br />
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>

<dd>
<h2><a id="Z" name="Z">Z</a></h2>
</dd>

<dt><a id="zinc-fingerprotein" name="zinc-fingerprotein"></a>
<b>Zinc-finger protein</b></dt>

<dd>A secondary feature of some proteins containing a zinc atom; a
DNA-binding protein. 
<p align="right"><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
</dd>
</dl>
